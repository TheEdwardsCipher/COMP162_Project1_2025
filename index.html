About the Dataset

According to UC Irvine’s website, the dataset StudentDataset was “created from a higher education institution [in Portugal] (acquired from several disjoint databases) related to students enrolled in different undergraduate degrees”. StudentDataset contains 4424 observations/rows and 37 variables/columns. Each observation represents a student’s response. Some variables include:

  • Marital status: nominal,
  • Application order: discreet,
  • Daytime/evening attendance: binary,
  • Previous qualification (grade): continuous,
  • Nationality: nominal,
  • Mother's qualification: ordinal,
  • Father's qualification: ordinal, and
  • Admission grade: continuous.

Motivation

This particular dataset was chosen for this project because it is (apparently) one of the most downloaded datasets on the site. It also piques my interest, since I want to become an educator (on top of being a mathematician) someday. Furthermore, it’s simply fun to play around with data!

Looking at the dataset, I can make some questions:

  1. Are there differences in Admission grade in the two binary variables Gender and Scholarship holder? What would happen if we break things down by International?
  2. Is there a correlation between Curricular units 1st sem (grade) and Curricular units 2nd sem (grade)? What would happen if we break things down by Target?

Part 1. Admission Grade

In Relation to Gender

We aim to see if there is a difference in Admission grade in terms of Gender. Because Gender is a binary variable – with only two choices Male and Female (much to the chagrin of non-binary individuals) – appropriate tests shall be utilised.

StudentDataset %>%
    ggplot(aes(x=Gender, y=`Admission grade`)) + 
    geom_boxplot()

#ggsave('Project 1, Part 1, Figure 1.png')

It seems interesting that the means of the two genders are rather close. We shall now administer a \(t\)-test.

t.test(`Admission grade`~Gender, data=StudentDataset)
## 
##  Welch Two Sample t-test
## 
## data:  Admission grade by Gender
## t = -0.5412, df = 2999, p-value = 0.5884
## alternative hypothesis: true difference in means between group Female and group Male is not equal to 0
## 95 percent confidence interval:
##  -1.1656588  0.6613672
## sample estimates:
## mean in group Female   mean in group Male 
##             126.8894             127.1416

We see that \(p = 0.5884 > \alpha\). This means we can accept the null hypothesis: that there is no substantial difference in admission grade between the two genders.

Part 2. Grades in 1st and 2nd Semesters

We aim to see if there is a correlation between Curricular units 1st sem (grade) and Curricular units 2nd sem (grade), both of which are numerical variables. We shall start by simply plotting these variables.

StudentDataset %>%
    ggplot(aes(x=`Curricular units 1st sem (grade)`, y=`Curricular units 2nd sem (grade)`)) +
    geom_point()

#ggsave('Project 1, Part 2, Figure 1.png')

We can see that there are many outliers (namely those 0 grades); for the sake of simplicity, those entries will be removed. To aid what we’re about to do later, let us also put a linear regression line.

StudentDatasetNoZeroGrade <- StudentDataset %>%
    filter(`Curricular units 1st sem (grade)` >= 10,
           `Curricular units 2nd sem (grade)` >= 10)

StudentDatasetNoZeroGrade %>%
    ggplot(aes(x=`Curricular units 1st sem (grade)`,
               y=`Curricular units 2nd sem (grade)`)) +
    geom_point() +
    geom_smooth(method="lm")
## `geom_smooth()` using formula = 'y ~ x'

#ggsave('Project 1, Part 2, Figure 2.png')

Much better! Though, I find it funny how the regression line actually does not rest on the diagonal \(y=x\) axis where many of the points line up. Now let us analyse this (filtered) dataset by checking its linear model and correlation coefficients.

We see that \(p = 2.2 \cdot 10^{-16}\), so the null hypothesis is rejected. Though, we also see that the residual standard error is \(1.011\) and \(R^2 = 0.4566\) (so about \(45%\) of variability in Curricular units 2nd sem (grade) is explained by the model); that’s not exactly terrible, but it’s not that good, either. The linear regression line is \[Y = 3.84892 + 0.69781 X + \varepsilon\] meaning for every 1 point increase in Curricular units 1st sem (grade), Curricular units 2nd sem (grade) increases \(0.69781\).
